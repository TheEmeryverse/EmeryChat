## Executive Summary
Meta has solidified its position as a major hyperscaler player through massive infrastructure commitments, including a **$35.2 billion total agreement with CoreWeave** (combining a $14.2B prior deal plus an additional $21B) and a **$27 billion five-year deal with Nebius**. The initial reporting of a "$48 billion" Nebius deal has been debunked; confirmed terms indicate $12 billion of dedicated capacity on NVIDIA's Vera Rubin platform (beginning early 2027), with an optional $15 billion in flexible compute purchases. To monetize its staggering **$125–$145 billion annual AI infrastructure spend**, Meta launched "Meta Compute" in July 2026, offering both Model-as-a-Service (MaaS) and bare-metal infrastructure to compete with AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud [S8][S18]. This deal signals a shift from pure internal AI research consumption to active cloud infrastructure landlordship, intensifying the broader hyperscaler arms race.

## Timeline
* **Early 2027:** CoreWeave and Nebius infrastructure contracts begin delivery of dedicated compute capacity on NVIDIA Vera Rubin platforms [S19][S8].
* **March 17, 2026:** Meta announces the $27 billion Nebius agreement, with Nebius stock rising ~14% following the news [S21].
* **April 9, 2026:** Expanded CoreWeave deal formally announced (total commitment raised to $35.2 billion across 2027–2032) [S8][S11].
* **May 27, 2026:** Mark Zuckerberg proposes the concept of monetizing surplus compute during Meta's shareholder meeting [S17].
* **July 2026:** "Meta Compute" cloud initiative officially launches, offering MaaS and bare-metal infrastructure to third parties [S18][P4].
* **2025–2032:** Multi-year hardware deployment cycles aligned with Meta's $125B–$145B annual spend and the broader hyperscaler capex wave [S17][P2].

## Key Actors
* **Meta Platforms:** Aggressively expanding AI infrastructure budget to $125B–$145B (2026) while pivoting toward a "cloud landlord" strategy via Meta Compute [S18][P4].
* **CoreWeave:** AI-native cloud provider partnering with Meta for $35.2 billion in dedicated GPU capacity; stock rose 24% YTD following the expanded deal [S8].
* **Nebius Group:** Allied AI cloud company securing a $27B, five-year contract tied to NVIDIA Vera Rubin hardware; plans to deploy 5GW of AI capacity by 2030 alongside a separate $2B NVIDIA partnership [S19][S21].
* **NVIDIA:** Supplies critical infrastructure (Vera Rubin platform) underpinning both the CoreWeave and Nebius deals, cementing its hardware dominance in the hyperscaler supply chain [S19][S21].
* **Hyperscaler Rivals (AWS, Azure, GCP):** Facing intensified competition as Meta shifts from pure compute consumer to direct market competitor [S4][S13].

## Core Findings
1. **Confirmed Deal Terms:** The reported "$48B Nebius deal" is inaccurate; confirmed figures are a $27B five-year agreement ($12B dedicated, $15B optional) starting early 2027, while Meta's cumulative CoreWeave commitment totals $35.2 billion (2027–2032) [S8][S19].
2. **Monetization Strategy:** Through "Meta Compute," Meta aims to sell unused AI compute and access to its models (e.g., Llama/Muse Spark), offsetting the massive infrastructure overhead associated with maintaining global AI operations [S18][P3].
3. **Hyperscaler CapEx Arms Race Acceleration:**
    * AWS: 2024 capex at ~$75B–$83B; projected to reach $100B in 2025, with AI-driven growth reaccelerating [S26][S27].
    * Microsoft: FY2024 capex reached $56B; Azure continues to grow (29% Q4 2024), though investors question monetization clarity [S40][S35].
    * Google: Estimated ~$48B–$75B in annualized spend depending on the reporting cycle [S35][S37].
    * Meta: Raising its own annual capex ceiling to $125B–$145B, representing unprecedented spending by a non-traditional cloud vendor [S18][P4].

## Competing Interpretations
* **Cloud Landlord vs. Model Seller:** Meta Compute reportedly offers both raw bare-metal infrastructure and Model-as-a-Service (MaaS). The choice between these models affects its competitive positioning, as it could directly rival neoclouds like CoreWeave (bare-metal) while leveraging Llama/Muse Spark to compete with OpenAI/Google AI [S18][P4].
* **Strategic Diversification vs. Vendor Lock-in:** Meta's multi-vendor strategy (CoreWeave, Nebius, and planned in-house builds) reduces single-point-of-failure risks but complicates unified fleet management compared to vertical integration seen at AWS or Azure [S8][P3].
* **AI Monetization Skepticism:** While Microsoft investors openly question how a $56B+ AI capex will yield returns due to sluggish Copilot adoption, Meta's massive spending is similarly scrutinized; however, its sheer internal demand for training and inference provides a baseline of guaranteed utilization absent at Microsoft [S40][P3].
* **Market Reaction Discrepancy:** Reports of Meta Compute have historically triggered varied market reactions—stock up over 10% on some reports due to monetization potential, while CoreWeave stock saw sharper gains purely based on volume contract security [S4][S8].

## Uncertainty & Confidence Notes
* **Source Reliability (Confirmed vs. Speculative):** The $27B Nebius figure is drawn from direct corporate press releases and financial filings (high reliability), whereas the initial $48B reports were unverified market rumors [S19][P2]. 
* **Pricing Mechanics:** Meta Compute's exact pricing for third-party bare-metal or model access remains undisclosed. Market impact on startup compute costs cannot yet be quantified [S18][P4].
* **Silicon Mix Gaps:** While NVIDIA Vera Rubin is confirmed as the platform, specific details on other GPU generations (H100/B-series/Blackwell) deployed across Meta's sprawling infrastructure are not fully disclosed in publicly available reporting [P9].
* **CapEx Guidance vs. Reality:** Hyperscaler capex projections (especially Amazon's $100B and Meta's $145B ceiling) depend heavily on power procurement, land acquisition, and hardware supply constraints that may delay or scale these numbers down [S26][S4].
