# Executive Summary

The U.S. Supreme Court issued a definitive ruling on June 30, 2026, striking down President Trump’s January 2025 executive order that sought to condition birthright citizenship on parental immigration status. In a 6‑3 decision authored by Chief Justice John Roberts, the Court reaffirmed that the Fourteenth Amendment’s Citizenship Clause unconditionally grants U.S. citizenship to anyone born on American soil, regardless of whether parents are undocumented or hold temporary visas. The opinion anchors its reasoning in *United States v. Wong Kim Ark* (1898) and plain statutory-text interpretation, closing a direct executive challenge while confirming that only an Article V constitutional amendment or a future judicial reversal can alter the principle.

---

## Timeline

1. **1868** – Fourteenth Amendment ratified; Citizenship Clause established to overturn *Dred Scott* and guarantee birthright citizenship [S22][S16].
2. **1898** – *United States v. Wong Kim Ark* cements the principle, extending it to children of permanent resident/non-citizen immigrants [S4][S13].
3. **1952** – Immigration and Nationality Act codifies statutory birthright citizenship at 8 U.S.C. § 1401(a) [S17][S18].
4. **Jan 20, 2025** – Executive Order 14156 signed, attempting to deny citizenship to children of unauthorized/visa-holding mothers and non-citizen fathers [S24].
5. **Jun 27, 2025** – SCOTUS rules on injunctive scope (*CASA v. Trump*), curtailing nationwide injunctions but permitting class‑based ones; a lower-court class injunction subsequently blocks the EO on July 10 [S12].
6. **Jun 30, 2026** – Supreme Court delivers final merits ruling, permanently striking down the executive order and reaffirming the 14th Amendment guarantee [S1][S13].

---

## Key Actors

- **Chief Justice John Roberts** – Drafted the majority opinion; invoked *Wong Kim Ark* and textualist reading of “subject to the jurisdiction thereof” [S4][S13].
- **Justices Thomas, Gorsuch, Alito** – Authored the principal dissent, arguing Framers’ original intent required exclusive political allegiance for full jurisdiction [S2][S13].
- **Justice Kavanaugh** – Concurred/dissented narrowly; held the EO violated the INA rather than the Constitution and pointed to legislative reform [S4].
- **Republican Legislative Leadership (Lee, Johnson, Cotton, Cornyn, Scott)** – Pushing a constitutional amendment post-ruling to restrict birthright citizenship through statute [S19].
- **22 States & City Plaintiffs** – Filed early federal challenges; ACLU and immigration councils backed the litigation pipeline [S10].

---

## Core Findings

- The 14th Amendment’s Citizenship Clause has been uniformly interpreted since 1898 to include children of undocumented and temporary-visa parents, excluding only foreign sovereigns and diplomats [S13][S23].
- Executive orders cannot override constitutional text; altering birthright citizenship requires either an Article V amendment (2/3 Congress + 3/4 states) or a new controlling SCOTUS ruling [S5][S19].
- The executive order would have impacted ~250,000 annual births; public polling places 70% of Americans in favor of maintaining the current rule [S1].
- Post-2025 injunctive precedent restricts lower federal courts from issuing nationwide blocks against executive actions, but class-specific injunctions remain legally permissible [S12].

---

## Competing Interpretations

1. **Majority (Textualist/Historical):** The Citizenship Clause is unambiguous. “Subject to the jurisdiction” excludes only foreign sovereigns and diplomats, not undocumented residents who are fully amenable to U.S. criminal and civil law [S13].
2. **Dissent (Originalist/Allegiance-Based):** The Framers tied citizenship to exclusive political allegiance. Children of unauthorized entrants lack the constitutional sense of “jurisdiction,” making the EO a permissible exercise of removal and nationality authority rather than a constitutional violation [S2][S13].
3. **Statutory/Pragmatic (Kavanaugh):** The real violation is statutory under the INA, not constitutional; Congress holds plenary power over nationality and can legislate changes without triggering an amendment [S4].
